    \graytext{building the control unit by storing \\ its control signals in memory}
  \end{center}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  Each step of an instruction needs a particular set of control signals. There are two ways to produce them.\\[3mm]
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \highlight{\textbf{hardwired control}}: \graytext{the signals come from a fixed circuit of gates} \\[2mm]
    \item \highlight{\textbf{microprogrammed control}}: \graytext{the signals are read from a small memory}
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Microinstructions}
  \begin{center}
    \Large \highlight{\textbf{microinstructions}}
  \end{center}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  A \highlight{\textbf{microinstruction}} is one stored word whose bits are the control signals for a single step.\\[5mm]
  \begin{figure}
    \centering
    \begin{tikzpicture}
      \foreach \b/\x in {1/0, 0/0.8, 1/1.6, 1/2.4, 0/3.2} {
        \node[draw, thick, minimum size=8mm, inner sep=0pt] at (\x,0) {\ttab{\b}};
      }
      \draw[decorate, decoration={brace, amplitude=5pt}, gray] (-0.45,-0.6) -- (3.65,-0.6);
      \node[smallgraytext] at (1.6,-1.1) {one bit per control signal};
    \end{tikzpicture}
  \end{figure}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  The microinstructions live in a \highlight{\textbf{control store}}, a small read-only memory. A counter steps through them, one per clock.\\[2mm]
  \begin{figure}
    \centering
    \begin{tikzpicture}[>=stealth]
      \node[draw, thick, fill=gray!30!white!20, minimum width=1.7cm, minimum height=1.0cm, align=center] (mpc) at (0,0) {micro\\ counter};
      \node[draw, thick, fill=gray!30!white!20, minimum width=2.0cm, minimum height=1.0cm, align=center] (cs) at (3.3,0) {control\\ store};
      \draw[thick, ->] (mpc) -- (cs);
      \draw[thick, ->] (cs.east) -- ++(1.2,0) node[right, smallgraytext] {control signals};
      % increment loop on the micro counter
      \draw[thick, ->] (mpc.south) -- ++(0,-0.8) -| ($(mpc.south)+(-1.15,0)$) -- (mpc.west);
      \node[smallgraytext] at (-1.5,-0.8) {\small$+1$};
    \end{tikzpicture}
  \end{figure}
  \begin{center}
    \graytext{reading the store in order replays a fixed sequence of signals}
  \end{center}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  Each machine instruction becomes a short sequence of microinstructions. Here is a simple add, one row per step.\\[4mm]
  \begin{center}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
    \begin{tabular}{r@{\hspace{8mm}}ccc}
                        & \graytext{read} & \graytext{add} & \graytext{write} \\ \hline
      \graytext{fetch}   & \cmark & \xmark & \xmark \\
      \graytext{decode}  & \xmark & \xmark & \xmark \\
      \graytext{execute} & \xmark & \cmark & \cmark \\
    \end{tabular}
  \end{center}
  \vspace{2mm}
  \begin{center}
    \graytext{the whole set of these sequences is the microprogram}
  \end{center}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  The two approaches trade speed against flexibility.\\[5mm]
  \begin{center}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.5}
    \begin{tabular}{r@{\hspace{8mm}}c@{\hspace{10mm}}c}
                            & \highlight{\textbf{hardwired}} & \highlight{\textbf{microprogrammed}} \\ \hline
      \graytext{built from} & \graytext{gates} & \graytext{a stored table} \\
      \graytext{speed}      & \graytext{faster} & \graytext{slower} \\
      \graytext{to change}  & \graytext{redesign logic} & \graytext{rewrite the store} \\
    \end{tabular}
  \end{center}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  \begin{center}
    Microprogramming turns the design of control into writing a table. The instructions it carries out are defined by the assembly language, which we cover next.
  \end{center}
\end{frame}
